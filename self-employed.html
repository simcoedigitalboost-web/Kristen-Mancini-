Self-Employed Mortgages

Your income is real. Let's prove it.

Banks don't always understand business owners. Your tax returns might show a fraction of what you actually earn—and that's smart accounting. I work with lenders who get it.

The self-employed challenge

You've built something real. Your business generates solid income and you're more than capable of handling a mortgage payment. But when you sit down with a traditional lender, they only see what's on your Notice of Assessment—and that number was designed to minimize taxes, not maximize borrowing power.

I specialize in finding lenders who look at the full picture: bank statements, contracts, business financials, and real cash flow. Not just the bottom line on your T1.

Income averaging

Some lenders will average your income over 2-3 years, which helps if you've had a strong recent year.

Stated income programs

For established business owners, certain lenders offer programs that rely more on business strength than personal tax returns.

Business-for-self (BFS)

Specific mortgage products designed for entrepreneurs with flexible documentation requirements.

Add-back programs

Lenders that allow legitimate business expenses to be "added back" to your qualifying income.

What you'll typically need

Documentation requirements vary by lender and program, but here's what I usually ask self-employed clients to gather:

  • 2 years of T1 Generals with NOAs
  • Business license or articles of incorporation
  • 6-12 months of business bank statements
  • Recent financial statements (if available)
  • Proof of HST/GST registration
  • Contracts or invoices showing ongoing work

Why work with a broker?

Your bank has one set of lending criteria. If you don't fit their box, you're out of luck. I have access to over 50 lenders—including several that specialize in self-employed borrowers.

More importantly, I know which lender to approach for your specific situation. A contractor with variable income needs a different solution than a consultant with steady retainers. A business owner with a corporation has different options than a sole proprietor.

I'll help you understand your options and find the lender whose policies work in your favor—not against you.
